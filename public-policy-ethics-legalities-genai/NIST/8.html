MAP: Context and Risk Identification
NIST AI RMF | MAP | "What can go wrong?"
MAP starts with WHY. Before building or deploying any AI system, you must understand the problem it solves, who it affects, and what could go wrong.
MAP One-Liner: "What can go wrong?"

The Three Core Questions of MAP

Why?

What problem is the AI solving? Use case must be clear.

Who?

Who gets affected? Internal users, external users, indirect stakeholders.

What could go wrong?

Proactively list potential failures, biases, and security threats.

Risk Identification — The Heart of MAP

The core question: "If this AI fails, how will it fail?"

The Four Dimensions of Strategic Context Mapping

Data Context

Source and Quality

What it examines: Where is data coming from? Is it clean, complete, representative? Does it carry historical bias?

Why it matters: Bad data reduces accuracy and creates system risks. Historical bias becomes automated discrimination.

"Garbage in = dangerous out"

Model Context

Design and Logic

What it examines: How is the model designed? Is it explainable? What are its limitations?

Why it matters: Complex models increase performance but reduce transparency. Black boxes create regulatory and trust risks.

Performance vs. Explainability trade-off

Business Context

Goals and Value

What it examines: What is the business objective? What is the cost of failure? Is risk justified for value created?

Why it matters: AI should not be deployed for problems where failure cost exceeds value.

Risk vs. Value assessment

Risk Context

Legal and Ethical

What it examines: Regulatory exposure, compliance requirements, ethical implications.

Why it matters: This is where AI decisions move from technical to societal. Regulatory non-compliance causes fines and operational freezes.

Technical → Societal

Which Contexts Do Companies Underestimate the Most?

Data Context and Risk Context (tied for most underestimated)

  • Data Context: Companies assume data is neutral; focus on quantity over quality; bias is invisible
  • Risk Context: Technical teams focus on technical risks; legal and technical silos; regulatory landscape is new

Consequence: AI systems amplify historical discrimination, and companies face regulatory fines and reputational destruction.

Stakeholder Impact Analysis

This is where questions of fairness, bias, and ethical impact begin to emerge.

Internal Users

Employees, managers, executives

Productivity, job displacement, trust in AI

External Users

Customers, clients, patients

Fairness, transparency, consent, harm prevention

Indirect Stakeholders

Communities, society, regulators, competitors

Systemic impact, market fairness, regulatory compliance

Key Insight: Different stakeholders have different vulnerabilities. Indirect stakeholders (regulators) often have the power to impose consequences.

1. Explain the four dimensions of strategic context mapping in MAP.
Answer:
Data Context: Source, quality, completeness, historical bias. Bad data reduces accuracy; historical bias becomes automated discrimination.
Model Context: Design, explainability, limitations. Complex models increase performance but reduce transparency.
Business Context: Goals, value, cost of failure. Determines whether risk is justified for value created.
Risk Context: Legal requirements, compliance, ethical implications. Where AI moves from technical to societal.
2. Why is stakeholder impact analysis critical in MAP?
Answer: Stakeholder impact analysis is critical because this is where questions of fairness, bias, and ethical impact begin to emerge.

Internal users: Employees, managers—productivity, job displacement
External users: Customers, patients—fairness, transparency, harm prevention
Indirect stakeholders: Society, regulators—systemic impact, compliance
Different stakeholders have different vulnerabilities. Indirect stakeholders (regulators) often have the power to impose consequences.
3. What does "risk identification is the heart of MAP" mean? What questions should be asked?
Answer: Risk identification is the heart of MAP because before deploying AI, you must understand what could go wrong. The core question: "If this AI fails, how will it fail?"

Proactive questions by category:
• Technical: What if accuracy drops? System crashes?
• Bias: Will AI treat groups unfairly? What proxy variables cause discrimination?
• Security: Can AI be attacked? Data poisoning?
• Operational: What happens at scale? Millions of wrong decisions?
• Reputational: What if AI is perceived as unfair?
• Regulatory: What laws might be violated?
4. Which strategic contexts do companies underestimate the most? Why?
Answer: Companies underestimate Data Context and Risk Context most frequently.

Why Data Context is underestimated:
• "Data is just data" fallacy—assume data is neutral, ignore historical bias
• Focus on quantity over quality—more data seen as better
• Bias is invisible—requires active investigation
• "Garbage in = dangerous out" not understood

Why Risk Context is underestimated:
• Technical teams focus on technical risks, not legal/ethical
• Legal and technical silos—legal not involved early
• Regulatory landscape is new—EU AI Act not integrated
• Societal impact is abstract—harder to quantify

Consequence: AI systems amplify historical discrimination, and companies face regulatory fines and reputational destruction.
5. How does MAP enable other NIST functions?
Answer:
GOVERN: MAP identifies risks that GOVERN must set policies and risk appetite for. Without MAP, GOVERN has no context.
MEASURE: MAP identifies what needs to be measured—bias, accuracy, security, drift. Without MAP, MEASURE has no framework.
MANAGE: MAP identifies risks that MANAGE must implement controls for. Without MAP, MANAGE doesn't know what to monitor.

Key Insight: MAP is the diagnostic phase. Without proper diagnosis, treatment (MEASURE, MANAGE) and oversight (GOVERN) cannot be effective.
