Introducing the NIST AI Risk Management Framework
NIST AI RMF 1.0 | Voluntary | Risk-Based | Iterative
What is NIST AI RMF? A voluntary framework developed by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology to help organizations manage AI risks and promote trustworthy AI.

NIST History & Framework Launch

NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) was founded on March 3, 1901 as the National Bureau of Standards (NBS) — one of the oldest physical science laboratories in the United States. It was renamed to NIST in 1988.

The Four Core Functions of NIST AI RMF

GOVERN

"Set the Rules"
Establish organizational governance structures, policies, and accountability for AI risk management. Define roles, responsibilities, and risk appetite. Ensure leadership oversight and compliance frameworks.

Who is accountable? What are the rules?

MAP

"Know Your Risks"
Understand the context, stakeholders, and potential risks. Identify AI system scope, data sources, intended use, and possible harms. Document assumptions and establish risk tolerance.

What could go wrong? Who is affected?

MEASURE

"Quantify Them"
Assess, analyze, and track AI risks using quantitative and qualitative methods. Test for bias, accuracy, security, and performance. Establish metrics and monitoring systems.

How much risk? How to track it?

MANAGE

"Control Them"
Implement controls, mitigation strategies, and incident response. Continuously monitor, update, and improve. Document decisions and maintain audit trails.

How to fix it? How to respond?

Iterative Lifecycle: The functions are not linear. They form a continuous loop—GOVERN enables MAP, which feeds MEASURE, which informs MANAGE, which loops back to refine GOVERN.
Which Function Do Companies Struggle With Most and Why?

GOVERN — Setting the Rules

Most organizations struggle with GOVERN because:

  • Unclear Accountability: No one wants to be personally responsible for AI outcomes. Organizations diffuse responsibility across teams, leaving no clear owner.
  • No Defined Risk Appetite: Leaders haven't decided what constitutes acceptable AI risk. Without thresholds, teams don't know when to stop or intervene.
  • Organizational Silos: Technical teams build AI; legal, compliance, and business teams are often excluded until problems emerge.
  • Leadership Ambiguity: Boards and executives treat AI as a technical matter rather than a governance imperative. No one at the top owns AI risk.
  • Cultural Resistance: "Move fast" cultures resist governance as slowing innovation. Without leadership mandate, governance is deprioritized.

Why This Matters: Without GOVERN, the other functions (MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE) cannot operate effectively. If no one is accountable, no one ensures risks are mapped, measured, or managed. Governance is the foundation—without it, the entire framework collapses.

1. When was NIST founded? When was the AI RMF launched?
Answer: NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) was founded on March 3, 1901 as the National Bureau of Standards (NBS). It was renamed to NIST in 1988. The AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) Version 1.0 was launched in January 2023 after development beginning in 2019 and a public draft released in March 2022.
2. Who is NIST AI RMF designed for? Why is it described as voluntary, risk-based, and iterative?
Answer: Designed for AI developers, business leaders, risk managers, regulators, and organizations of all sizes across all sectors.

Voluntary: Not legally mandated; organizations choose to adopt it as a best practice.
Risk-based: Focuses on severity and likelihood of harms rather than prescriptive rules.
Iterative: Risk management is a continuous cycle, not a one-time checklist. The four functions loop continuously throughout the AI lifecycle.
3. Explain the four core functions of NIST AI RMF: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE.
Answer:

GOVERN — "Set the Rules": Establish organizational governance structures, policies, accountability, and risk appetite. Define roles and ensure leadership oversight.

MAP — "Know Your Risks": Understand context, stakeholders, and potential risks. Identify AI system scope, data sources, and possible harms.

MEASURE — "Quantify Them": Assess, analyze, and track AI risks using quantitative methods. Test for bias, accuracy, security, and performance.

MANAGE — "Control Them": Implement controls, mitigation strategies, and incident response. Continuously monitor and maintain audit trails.
4. Which NIST AI RMF function do companies struggle with most? Why?
Answer: Most organizations struggle with GOVERN because:

• Unclear accountability: No one wants to be personally responsible for AI outcomes.
• No defined risk appetite: Leaders haven't decided acceptable risk thresholds.
• Organizational silos: Technical teams operate separately from legal, compliance, and business.
• Leadership ambiguity: Boards treat AI as technical rather than governance imperative.
• Cultural resistance: "Move fast" cultures resist governance as slowing innovation.

Why this matters: Without GOVERN, the other functions cannot operate effectively. If no one is accountable, no one ensures risks are mapped, measured, or managed. Governance is the foundation.
5. What are the seven characteristics of trustworthy AI according to NIST?
Answer: NIST defines seven characteristics of trustworthy AI:

1. Valid & Reliable: Performs consistently as intended
2. Safe: Minimizes harm to humans and society
3. Secure & Resilient: Protected from attacks and failures
4. Accountable & Transparent: Clear ownership and visibility into operations
5. Explainable & Interpretable: Decisions can be understood by humans
6. Privacy-Enhanced: Protects personal data
7. Fair with Harmful Bias Managed: Treats all groups equitably

Organizations should embed these characteristics throughout the AI lifecycle using the four functions.
