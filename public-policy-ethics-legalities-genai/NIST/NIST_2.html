The Three Critical Questions

1. Who is accountable?

Accountability always sits at the top. AI is not responsible. Leaders are! A named individual must answer for AI outcomes.

2. Can you explain the decision?

"Explained" is not an option anymore — it is a business requirement. Why was the loan rejected? Why was the candidate filtered out?

3. Can you defend it legally?

Requires traceability, documentation, and auditability. NIST AI RMF emphasizes these for legal defensibility.

The Governing Insight: If you cannot answer these three questions, you don't have a technical AI problem — you have a governance problem. Governance problems require leadership accountability, not just model fixes.

1. Explain why "AI is not responsible. Leaders are." What does this mean for organizational governance?
Answer: AI systems are tools—they cannot be held legally or ethically accountable for outcomes. Accountability always resides with human leaders. This means:

• Named individuals (C-suite, business unit heads, CAIRO) must be assigned responsibility for AI outcomes
• Accountability cannot be delegated to technical teams or hidden behind "the algorithm made the decision"
• Leaders must personally answer for failures, biases, and harms caused by AI systems they deploy
• Governance structures must make accountability explicit and enforceable
2. Why is "explained" no longer optional for AI decisions? What does this mean for organizations?
Answer: "Explained" has become a business requirement because:

• Customers demand to know why they were rejected for loans, jobs, or services
• Regulators require explainability to assess compliance with anti-discrimination laws
• Courts require clear reasoning to evaluate fairness and legality
• Without explainability, organizations cannot build trust or defend decisions

Organizations must ensure their AI systems can produce human-understandable explanations for every decision, identifying which factors influenced the outcome and why.
3. What documentation is required to defend an AI decision legally? How does this relate to NIST AI RMF?
Answer: To defend an AI decision legally, organizations need:

• Training data sources and composition documentation
• Model development records and version control
• Testing and validation results
• Fairness metrics and bias assessments
• Complete audit logs of every decision
• Human oversight and intervention records

NIST AI RMF addresses this through:
• MEASURE: Quantifying and documenting performance, bias, and risks
• MANAGE: Establishing audit trails and incident response protocols
• GOVERN: Ensuring documentation practices are embedded in governance
4. What is the difference between an "AI problem" and a "governance problem"? Use the three questions to explain.
Answer:

AI problem (technical): Model accuracy, performance drift, or data quality issues that can be fixed with engineering resources.

Governance problem (systemic): The inability to answer:
• Who is accountable? (No clear ownership)
• Can you explain the decision? (No interpretability)
• Can you defend it legally? (No documentation or traceability)

If these questions cannot be answered, the issue is not technical but structural. Governance problems require changes in leadership, accountability structures, and organizational processes—not just model improvements.
5. How do the three questions on Slide 3 map to the NIST AI RMF functions? Provide specific examples.
Answer:

Who is accountable? → GOVERN: Appoint a Chief AI Risk Officer with sign-off authority before deployment.

Can you explain the decision? → MAP: Conduct context mapping to ensure interpretability requirements are defined; use explainable AI techniques.

Can you defend it legally? → MEASURE & MANAGE: Track fairness metrics, maintain audit logs, document all decisions, establish incident response.

Together, these functions transform the ability to answer the three questions from a hope into a documented, auditable capability.
