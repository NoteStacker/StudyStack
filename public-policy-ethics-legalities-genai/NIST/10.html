The Three Components of MANAGE

Controls

Prevent Before Occurrence

Technical and process safeguards designed to mitigate risks before they materialize. Implementing specific mechanisms to mitigate identified risks before they occur.

Monitoring

Continuous Real-Time Oversight

Tracking model performance, drift, and unexpected behaviors post-deployment. A model that was safe yesterday may become risky tomorrow.

Incident Response

When Things Go Wrong

Pre-planned protocols for rollbacks, kill-switches, and stakeholder communication. Not "if" but "when."

Technical Controls

Process Controls

Human-led procedures and oversight

Examples: Approval workflows, audit requirements, human-in-the-loop review, documentation mandates, pre-deployment sign-off

2. Detection & AnalysisIdentify that incident is occurringMonitoring alerts; drift detection; user reports; anomaly detection

AI-Specific Considerations:
  • Time to Detect: AI failures can impact millions instantly; detection must be real-time
  • Time to Contain: Kill-switches must be one-click; rollback must be instantaneous
  • Communication Speed: Stakeholder communication must happen within hours, not days
  • Forensic Analysis: Need complete audit trails to understand why failure occurred

1. What are the three components of MANAGE? Explain each.
Answer:

Controls: Technical and process safeguards designed to mitigate risks before they materialize. Technical controls: bias mitigation algorithms, access restrictions. Process controls: approvals, audits, human-in-the-loop.

Monitoring: Continuous real-time oversight tracking model performance, drift, and unexpected behaviors post-deployment. A model that was safe yesterday may become risky tomorrow.

Incident Response: Pre-planned protocols for when things go wrong. Includes rollback procedures, kill-switches, stakeholder communication, root cause analysis, and remediation planning.
2. What is the difference between technical controls and process controls? Provide examples.
Answer:

Technical Controls: Automated safeguards built into the AI system. Examples: bias mitigation algorithms, input validation, access restrictions, rate limiting, output filtering, kill-switches.

Process Controls: Human-led procedures and oversight. Examples: approval workflows, audit requirements, human-in-the-loop review, documentation mandates, pre-deployment sign-off.

Both are essential. Technical controls provide speed and automation; process controls provide judgment and accountability.
3. Why is continuous monitoring critical in MANAGE? What can happen without it?
Answer: Continuous monitoring is critical because AI systems evolve—data changes, user behavior changes, environment changes. A model that was safe yesterday may become risky tomorrow.

Without monitoring: Degradation goes unnoticed; failure occurs suddenly and catastrophically; root cause unknown; organizations react in crisis mode.

With monitoring: Alerts trigger when thresholds breached; proactive intervention before failure; drift detection identifies what changed; models retrained before they fail.
4. Describe the NIST Incident Response framework and how it applies to AI systems.
Answer: The NIST Incident Response Lifecycle has four phases:

Preparation: Establish capabilities before incident — pre-drafted communication; kill-switch testing; response team identified.
Detection & Analysis: Identify that incident is occurring — monitoring alerts; drift detection; user reports.
Containment, Eradication & Recovery: Stop incident and restore operations — rollback; kill-switch activation; model retraining.
Post-Incident Activity: Learn to prevent recurrence — root cause analysis; policy updates; documentation.

AI-specific considerations: Time to detect must be real-time; kill-switches must be one-click; communication must happen within hours; complete audit trails needed.
5. What does "MANAGE is about turning insight into action, strategy into operations, governance into control" mean?
Answer: This means MANAGE is where the entire NIST framework becomes operational:

Insight → Action: MAP identifies risks; MEASURE quantifies them; MANAGE does something about them.
Strategy → Operations: GOVERN sets policies and risk appetite; MANAGE implements them in day-to-day operations.
Governance → Control: Governance structures exist on paper; MANAGE creates actual mechanisms (controls, monitoring, response) that enforce them.

Without MANAGE, GOVERN, MAP, and MEASURE are theoretical. MANAGE makes them real.
