GOVERN

"Who owns it?"

Business Translation: Accountability and Ownership

Who is accountable? Who signs off before deployment? Who is responsible when things go wrong?

Executive Action: Appoint named owners; require executive sign-off; establish governance councils.

MAP

"What can go wrong?"

Business Translation: Context and Risk Identification

What is the AI intended to do? What could go wrong? Who will be affected? What are the assumptions?

Executive Action: Demand risk assessments; identify stakeholders; maintain risk register.

MEASURE

"How bad is the risk?"

Business Translation: KPIs and Validation Metrics

How do we track performance? What metrics define success? How do we detect bias or degradation?

Executive Action: Define KPIs; require bias, accuracy, reliability reporting; set thresholds.

MANAGE

"What do we do about it?"

Business Translation: Decision and Action Plans

What do we do when it fails? How do we respond? What controls are in place?

Executive Action: Prioritize risks; implement controls; monitor continuously; establish incident response.

Key KPIs Executives Must Demand

Metric CategoryWhat to MeasureWhy It Matters
Accuracy MetricsPrecision, recall, F1-score, AUC-ROC, error ratesHow often is the model correct?
Bias IndicatorsDisparate impact ratio, equal opportunity difference, demographic parityIs the model fair across demographic groups?
Reliability ThresholdsConfidence intervals, calibration scores, stability over timeHow confident can we be in outputs?
Performance DriftAccuracy degradation, concept drift detectionWhen does the model start failing?
Security MetricsAdversarial robustness, vulnerability scansIs the model secure from attacks?

The Four Components of MANAGE

Risk Prioritization

Rank risks by severity and likelihood; allocate resources accordingly

Control Implementation

Technical and process safeguards (human-in-the-loop, input validation)

Continuous Monitoring

Real-time oversight; define escalation triggers; review regularly

Incident Response

Pre-planned protocols: rollback, kill-switches, communication plans

The Measurement Imperative:

"What gets measured gets managed. What is not measured cannot be managed and becomes invisible risk."

"If you don't measure bias, you are accepting bias."

Without measurement, risks become invisible, unmanaged, and ultimately lead to failures.

1. Explain the four NIST AI RMF functions using the one-liner framework. What executive question does each answer?
Answer:
GOVERN: "Who owns it?" — Accountability and Ownership
MAP: "What can go wrong?" — Context and Risk Identification
MEASURE: "How bad is the risk?" — KPIs and Validation Metrics
MANAGE: "What do we do about it?" — Decision and Action Plans
This one-liner framework enables leaders to quickly grasp and apply NIST AI RMF functions.
2. What KPIs should executives demand under MEASURE? Why is measurement critical?
Answer: Executives must demand:
• Accuracy Metrics: Precision, recall, F1-score, error rates
• Bias Indicators: Disparate impact ratio, equal opportunity difference
• Reliability Thresholds: Confidence intervals, calibration scores
• Performance Drift: Accuracy degradation, concept drift detection

Why measurement is critical: "What gets measured gets managed. What is not measured cannot be managed and becomes invisible risk. If you don't measure bias, you are accepting bias."
3. What are the four components of MANAGE? Describe each.
Answer:
Risk Prioritization: Ranking risks by severity and likelihood; allocate resources where needed most.
Control Implementation: Technical and process safeguards (human-in-the-loop, input validation).
Continuous Monitoring: Real-time oversight; define escalation triggers; review regularly.
Incident Response: Pre-planned protocols for failures: rollback procedures, kill-switches, communication plans.
4. Why is MAP (Context and Risk Identification) critical before deployment?
Answer: MAP is critical because:
• Leaders must understand what the AI is intended to do
• Potential harms must be identified before they occur
• Stakeholders affected by failures must be known
• Assumptions and limitations must be documented

If MAP is skipped: Risks go unidentified until crisis hits; organizations deploy without understanding harms; no risk register exists to guide measurement and management.
5. Explain the statement: "What gets measured gets managed. If you don't measure bias, you are accepting bias."
Answer: This principle means measurement is essential for accountability:
• Without bias metrics, you cannot know if AI discriminates
• Without accuracy metrics, you don't know when the model fails
• Without drift detection, performance degradation goes unnoticed
• Invisible risks become unmanaged risks that cause failures

Application: Executives must demand measurement of bias, accuracy, and reliability. Lack of measurement does not absolve accountability—if you don't measure bias, you are implicitly accepting it.
